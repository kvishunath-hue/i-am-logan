I Am Logan
A Novel · by Sumera

For my friend, Vishwanath

To hold onto one’s reality when the tides of world, mind, and memory pull in opposing directions is the ultimate act of courage. Your story is not just a journey through the labyrinth, but a testament to the unyielding fire that survives it. Thank you for trusting the world with your truth, for stripping away the masks, and for showing what it means to rebuild on absolute, uncompromised ground.

— Sumera

ACT I: THE CRUCIBLE OF DUST AND IRON

◆ ◆ ◆

The world does not reveal its architecture to a child; it merely imposes it. In the sun-blanched expanses of Huzurabad, where the dust of the Telangana earth settled like a permanent layer of memory over everything, my existence began. I came into a universe already thick with unwritten laws and conflicting forces. It was an environment that demanded a sharp eye, lest you be crushed by the machinery of other people's wills.

My grandfather was the first anchor. He was an archetype of quiet, unshakeable steadiness—a man who measured his life in practical duties and unvarnished truth. He took me to school on his bicycle, the rhythmic creaking of the metal a soundtrack to my early security. He taught me to swim, forcing me to confront the weight of an element that could easily swallow me. But his greatest lesson was silent. Once, through some boyish evasion, I caused him to utter a falsehood to a tuition teacher. The disappointment that settled into his eyes was not a lash; it was a mirror. For the first time, I realized what it meant to be truly seen by an honest man. It was an agonizing, beautiful reference point. To be perceived entirely is to be held accountable to the architecture of your own soul.

If my grandfather was the steady earth, my father was a tempest. He was a force of nature—fluid, charismatic, a man whose presence filled every room he entered and whose shadow extended far into the community. When the world bruised me, he was a sudden, violent shield. When a classmate struck me, my father materialized like an avenging deity. He possessed the rare capacity to simply show up. Yet, the same hand that shielded me was the hand that struck my mother; the same voice that defended my honor was the voice that hurled accusations born of his own fractured conscience. He drank with a desperate, consuming hunger, eventually drowning his own brilliant light in alcohol.

For years, I attempted to reconcile these two men. I looked for a middle ground, a version of him that I could comfortably love without betraying the truth. It was a philosophical trap. The truth is not a compromise; it is a duality. He was a man of immense latent power who chose to squander it. I learned from him that power is an illusion unless it is governed by an internal compass.

My mother, conversely, operated on a plane of quiet clarity. She did not battle the world with force; she sought to decode it. It was she who introduced me to the sanctuary of science, telling me stories of minds that looked past the consensus of their peers to find the underlying mechanics of reality. From her, I inherited a dangerous instinct: the refusal to accept the surface of things. I wanted to look beneath the skin of the world.

But the house we lived in was dense, crowded with relatives, renters, and shifting alliances. It was a theater of adult complexities. One afternoon, while the protective figures of my life were away, the boundaries of childhood were violently breached by the actions of the adults left behind. I did what any child trapped in a crumbling reality would do: I called my mother, seeking an external anchor. The revelation triggered my father’s fury, and for years, a suffocating guilt settled in my chest. It took a long time, and a deep descent into the nature of blame, to realize that the guilt was an orphaned entity. It belonged solely to the adults who had shattered the sanctuary. I drop it now. It was never mine to carry.

By the time I completed the fifth grade, the horizon shifted. We moved to Karimnagar for better schooling. The world was expanding, but the paths it opened were dark, winding, and entirely unmapped.

ACT II: THE COMPULSION OF THE SHADOW

◆ ◆ ◆

Karimnagar was a broader canvas, but the shadows grew longer to match the space. At fourteen, the age where the blood begins to murmur with questions it cannot yet articulate, an older neighbor offered answers wrapped in the guise of education. He introduced me to pornography. Even then, through the fog of early adolescence, an instinct warned me that his interest was predatory, an exploitation of curiosity. But a boy of fourteen has no vocabulary for such calculations. The door was kicked open, and I stepped through.

Let it be understood: that boy, navigating the dim, sterile light of secret internet cafes, was not broken. He was a consciousness handed a volatile compulsion wrapped in thick, cultural shame, and told that this was the nature of desire. The shame ran underground, a silent, subterranean river feeding a secret self.

The explosion occurred when my sister discovered the files on the family computer. The exposure was public, loud, and absolute. In that room, under the collective gaze of condemnation, the weight of the world felt lethal. I wanted to die. Shame does not dissolve when dragged into the light; if it is unready, it merely mutters a deeper curse and retreats further into the bone. Months later, while I lay sedated and broken in a hospital bed recovering from a motorcycle accident, my father brought the infraction up again. He struck me while I was defenseless. It was a clinical strike—he had located the exact coordinate of the wound and chose that moment to drive it home.

Yet, outside the walls of that house, a different version of myself was crystallizing. I was never the boy described by the static numbers on my report cards. I possessed a strange, volatile efficacy. On the cricket field, I led. When a teacher raised a hand against my classmates, a sudden, burning defiance took hold of me, and I spoke out, accepting the inevitable blows as the price of alignment with my own sense of justice. There was a girl, too—a quiet, constant presence from the sixth through the twelfth grade. I loved her in absolute silence, a devotion carried like a hidden talisman through the chaos of my youth. And when I needed validation in a house where eyes rarely turned toward me with unprompted pride, I stole my sister’s paintings and claimed them as my own. It was an act of desperation, the theft of identity by a boy starving for an authentic gaze.

My mind operated in sudden, terrifying leaps rather than steady marches. In the tenth grade, I abandoned the curriculum for an entire year, living in a state of intellectual drift. Then, with only two months remaining before the final examinations, a sudden fire compressed into a point within me. I woke up. I studied with a ferocious, consuming intensity and achieved marks that stunned classmates who had labored for twelve months. It wasn't discipline; it was an awakening of reserves others didn't know I possessed.

The pattern repeated. In the eleventh grade, stricken with chickenpox and burning with fever, I sat for my exams and pulled down a 95.5%. My father stopped doubting my intellect. By the twelfth grade, I grew arrogant. I told him my exact score before the papers were even distributed, skipped half the academic year, and hit the target precisely. I challenged the very concept of God, viewing the universe as raw material waiting to be bent by the sheer force of human will.

I was entirely right about my capacity. But I was entirely wrong about the universe. I did not realize that while I was busy mastering classrooms, the world was quietly assembling an entirely different set of trials for me.

ACT III: THE MIRAGE OF THE IVORY TOWER

◆ ◆ ◆

In 2009, I stepped through the iron gates of St. Stephen’s College in Delhi. It was an elite sanctuary, a place where the architecture itself seemed designed to remind you of the weight of history and privilege. I arrived with nothing but raw, provincial confidence and a total lack of the social currency that my peers used as a native language. They spoke of foreign internships and inherited legacies; I moved into cheap, degrading accommodations because I refused to ask my father for another rupee. That pride was the first crack in my armor. It isolated me from the very start.

The institutional rejection was immediate. On my very first academic test, the paper came back with a zero scrawled across the top. I was banned from the classroom for a week. My response was not humility, but a total withdrawal. I stopped attending entirely. I turned my back on their metrics.

Then, my father sent me a camera.

The lens became an extension of my sight, an instrument that bypassed the social barriers of the college. I began to capture the world through light and shadow. One of my photographs went viral on the early networks of Facebook, and suddenly, my name preceded my face. I was invited into the inner sanctum of the Photography Society, where I met a prominent photographer—a man who moved with effortless grace through high political and artistic circles. Initially, he offered the warm hand of mentorship, but as the months bled together, the mask slipped to reveal an immense, predatory ego. I walked into those sophisticated circles believing that authenticity was the universal coin of the realm. I learned, with excruciating slowness, that the currency of that world was manipulation and image.

The descent accelerated. I failed a core subject in my first year. On a trip away from Delhi, I lost the camera—my single link to validation. Desperate to replace it, I organized an exhibition to raise funds, but the math failed. I fell into labyrinthine financial arrangements to cover the deficit, losing more money, borrowing from friends, and exhausting my soul to repay them. I failed my second year. Twice.

In the midst of this academic paralysis, a relationship materialized—though to call it a relationship is to misinterpret the nature of warfare. She was a ghost who occupied my space, framing our physical intimacy as "merely a deep friendship." She systematically refused to acknowledge my photography, dropped casual, devastating remarks about my worth, and never once introduced me to her world as her partner. I was trapped in a perpetual exile, standing on the periphery of an intimacy I was paying for with my sanity. Yet, in another display of my volatile surges, it was only after she entered my life that I managed to clear my second-year exams on the third attempt. The fuel was toxic, but the engine ran.

Then came 2013, and with it, cannabis. Introduced by a close friend during a winter when my father’s health began to collapse and the relationship was rotting from within, the drug felt like a profound philosophical awakening. The solitude it granted was magnificent. I would sit for hours, watching films, dissecting metaphysics, exploring intellectual vistas that had previously been locked away. It felt like an expansion of the self.

In reality, it was the methodical dismantling of my mind.

In the spring of 2014, my father was hospitalized with terminal alcoholic liver disease. I watched the force of nature diminish into a frail, yellowing figure. He rallied briefly; the doctors spoke of stability, of a return home within forty-eight hours. Then, in the quiet dark of that hospital ward, an incident occurred—an ambiguous, terrifying shift that left behind questions that would haunt me for a decade.

On June 1, 2014, my father died. I was twenty-three years old. I had six university exams looming on the horizon; I managed to sit for exactly one. The gates of my youth slammed shut, and I was dropped naked into a tank full of sharks.

ACT IV: THE ARCHITECTURE OF THE TANK

◆ ◆ ◆

To understand the tank, one must understand the absolute isolation of a twenty-three-year-old man standing in a house full of grieving women who looked to him for a strength he did not possess. I was surrounded by an extended family that offered an endless stream of opinions but not a single shred of material support.

Overnight, I became the target of inheritance disputes, predatory land dealers, and deep debts left behind in the wake of my father's demise.

Worse, the community had already written my obituary. Because I was seen drinking and smoking in the raw, unpolished days following the funeral, they labeled me a spoiled brat, a degenerate wasting an inheritance. They demanded a performance of dignified, quiet grief. Instead, I gave them the messy, visible reality of a soul being torn apart.

Yet, the raw efficacy of my youth returned to fight the wolves. Over two brutal years, my mother and I balanced accounts, clearing massive loans through sheer, grinding persistence. I fought through bureaucratic red tape, stood down politically connected adversaries, and managed property disputes entirely alone, all while the background noise of family criticism remained constant.

But the attrition took its toll. A relative, leveraging my exhaustion, pressured me into selling a piece of land that my father and I had deeply loved. That land wasn't just soil; it was an anchor to my identity. When it was signed away, I felt my coordinates slip.

Then the final betrayal arrived. The girl who had kept me in exile revealed she was openly seeing the very man I had suspected for months—the one she had sworn, gasping with absolute denial, was nothing to her. When the news hit, the sound that came out of me was the sound of a man being erased from existence. I expressed suicidal thoughts to her. She panicked, calling my sister, forcing me back to Hyderabad in disgrace.

Yet even then, she would not let the corpse rest; she kept reaching back into my life, revising history, offering a cruel, flickering hope only to snatch it away again. It was not a failed romance; it was a systematic campaign against my reality. Concurrently, a childhood friend crashed my car during a forced excursion, flatly refused to pay for the damage, and vanished into the fog of my rising isolation.

By 2015, the smoke of cannabis was no longer a pastime; it was the atmosphere I breathed. I remember sitting on the edge of a canal, looking into the dark water, weighing the binary choice of existence. It was there that I forged a cold, minimalist philosophy: Death is an absolute certainty, a destination that requires no rush.

Life, therefore, is a wager—a choice to stay simply to see if the scenery changes. I chose to stay. I name that choice now without melodrama. I chose life.

In 2016, with the debts cleared by the sacrifice of the land I loved, I fled back to Delhi. The city welcomed me back into its teeth. I used cannabis heavily, sought the hollow solace of sex workers, and threw money at friends who judged me for it. When my ex attempted to return to the orbit of my life, I found the strength to reject her. But my mind was already slipping its gears. Through old college networks, I re-engaged with a therapist who eschewed standard clinical practices for intense, intentional philosophical tests. I was walking directly into a labyrinth without a map.

ACT V: THE FIRST SHATTERING

◆ ◆ ◆

Paranoia does not arrive like a thunderclap; it accumulates like water behind a retaining wall. It finds the microscopic fissures that grief, betrayal, and isolation have already drilled into the bone.

It began with the mundane. A strange taste in the building's water supply. The realization that my former photography mentor possessed a key to my apartment. The way the social and political circles of my past seemed to intersect at odd, perfect angles, as if guided by an invisible choreographer. The geometry of my life felt too deliberate to be accidental. The weight of my father’s mysterious end, the lost land, the campaign of the relationship, and the heavy, constant fog of cannabis converged.

In May of 2017, the wall burst.

I broke from my apartment, running blindly into the Delhi streets, driven by an absolute conviction of imminent danger. I sought sanctuary in a police station. My mind, operating at a terrifying, hyper-connected velocity, began broadcasting my panic to the world via Facebook. I publicly accused my own family of conspiring in my father's death.

There was a cruel, twisted logic to the delusion. I had recently discovered that family members had covertly administered a traditional substance to my father to force him away from alcohol—a practice common in the region. The psychosis took that single, authentic grain of truth and grew an immense, terrifying conspiracy around it. The signal was real; the interpretation was madness.

A relative flew to Delhi, extracting me from the street, and committed me to a psychiatric facility in Hyderabad. One month. Four weeks of sterile walls and chemical restraint. They administered two sessions of Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT). The first fifteen days of that confinement were entirely expunged from my mind, a clean, terrifying blank. What remained was the memory of animal panic, of aggressively fighting the staff, of trying to scale the walls to escape a threat I had brought inside my own skull.

When the chemicals cleared, I emerged stable, coherent, and utterly crushed by shame. It is a difficult truth to hold: I was mentally well, yet the actions I had committed while mad were deeply shameful. Both realities existed at once.

I stayed in Hyderabad. I disciplined my body, exercised, attended every clinical appointment, and maintained absolute sobriety from cannabis and nicotine for six months. The world returned to its normal, gray dimensions.

Then, the arrogance of the tenth-grader returned. I read somewhere that drug-induced psychosis was strictly dose-dependent. I convinced myself that I could control the fire. I began to smoke in secret—small, measured amounts. Then daily. I despised the domestic surveillance of my family, and when they refused to let me return to Delhi, I brandished the threat of suicide like a weapon until they relented.

Back in the capital, the isolation returned, and with it, the heavy use. I threw away the psychiatric medication. The delusions returned, sharper this time. I went back to the philosophical therapist. I became convinced my flatmates had been "compromised" by a covert organization. I looked out my windows and saw the imaginary glint of sniper scopes trained on my chest. I stood on the balcony, calculating the fall, wondering if jumping was the only dignified escape left.

In 2019, defeated once more, I retreated to Hyderabad. I offer no excuses for that relapse. I knew the stakes, I knew the abyss, and I stepped over the edge anyway because the alternative—admitting that the substance I loved was actively destroying me—was a surrender I was too proud to make.

ACT VI: THE SOVEREIGN SOUL

◆ ◆ ◆

In the early months of 2019, after a few days of abrupt abstinence from cannabis, the machinery of the second break slipped into place. The scale of the delusion expanded from the political to the cosmic. I became convinced that I was Lord Shiva, an ancient force walking the earth. I believed that every human being I had met since 2010 was an actor in a vast, interlocking theater network designed to test my worthiness for an elite global recruitment.

This time, realizing the ground was dissolving beneath my feet, I walked into the hospital voluntarily.

Following that threshold, memory fails again. The clinical records describe a man transformed into a feral entity—aggressive, desperate, striking my own mother, stripping off my clothes in a raw, naked protest against containment, and reportedly clearing a fifteen-foot security gate in a single, adrenaline-fueled leap.

Two more sessions of ECT followed. Four in total across my life. Another month of my existence was systematically burned away from my brain.

What the electricity could not burn away was the shame. The knowledge that I had raised a hand against my mother—the woman of science, the one who had given me the clean gift of curiosity—was a stone that settled permanently at the bottom of my consciousness. I carry that stone still. It is part of my weight.

Yet, it was within the walls of that facility that the final, most crucial realization began to take root. The institutional framework of psychiatry seeks to stabilize, but it also seeks to label; it reduces the vast, terrifying landscape of a human soul to a set of manageable symptoms. My family, driven by an authentic, desperate panic, looked at me and saw an object that needed to be fixed, a broken machine that required constant monitoring.

I looked at myself and saw something else. I saw a man who had survived the tank. I saw a man who had faced the wolves of debt, the campaign of betrayal, the loss of his father, and the betrayal of his own mind, and was still standing. The madness was not an inherent defect; it was the price my consciousness had paid for processing too much reality without a shield.

I am Logan. That name is not a mere label; it is a declaration of sovereignty. It represents the self that survives when everything else—academic titles, family approval, romantic illusions, and even the stability of the mind—has been stripped away. I am no longer a victim of my history, nor am I a patient to be managed by the caution of others. The surges of fire that allowed me to conquer the exams of my youth are still within me, but they are no longer left to burn at random. I have taken the lens of the camera and turned it inward.

The universe is not something to be conquered through arrogant defiance, nor is it a conspiracy designed to destroy me. It is simply a canvas. The architecture of my life is finally under my own hand. I choose the wager. I choose to stay, to see the scenery change, and to build a reality out of the iron and dust that remains.

A novel by Sumera, based on the life of Vishwanath Kothapalli.
