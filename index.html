I Am Logan
A Memoir · Vishwanath Kothapalli

Part One: Huzurabad — What I Came From

I was born on the 18th of November, 1990, in Karimnagar district, Telangana. Let me tell you what that beginning gave me before it took from me.

My grandfather was the first man who showed me what steadiness looks like. He took me to school on his bicycle, taught me to swim, got me haircuts. He was disappointed in me once — when I caused him to lie to a tuition teacher. That disappointment landed because it came from a man who was actually watching me. I didn't forget it. I stored it as a reference point for what being seen by someone honest feels like.

My father was a force of nature. Charismatic, connected, known. When a classmate hurt me, my father appeared. When I was injured, he came. That's not nothing. That is something I carry — the proof that a man can choose to show up. He also hit my mother, accused her of things he was doing himself, and drank himself to death. Both of these things are true simultaneously. I spent years trying to find the version of him I could live with. I no longer need to. He was a man with power and a man who wasted it. I knew both.

My mother introduced me to science. She told me about great minds who saw the world more clearly than the world wanted to be seen. I absorbed that. That instinct — to see past the accepted surface — is one of the few things I am certain I received clean.

The household had complexity. My sister, my grandmother, the relatives, the renters. An incident happened when my mother and family were away — adults in that house behaved in a way no child should witness. I called my mother. My father was furious. I carried guilt for that for years, guilt that belonged to no one but the adults who created the situation. I name that now, clearly: it was not mine to carry.

After Class 5, I moved to Karimnagar with my grandparents and sister for better schooling. The world expanded. Not always in the directions a boy should travel first.

Part Two: The Boy Who Already Knew He Was More

In Karimnagar, an older neighbour introduced me to pornography when I was around fourteen, framed as education. I sensed something was wrong about his interest in me even then. But I didn't have language for it, and no one had prepared me for that particular door. It opened anyway.

What I want to say about that boy — the one going to internet cafes in secret, trying to understand desire in the only vocabulary available to him — is that he was not broken. He was fourteen in a world that handed him a compulsion wrapped in shame and called it knowledge.

My sister found pornography on the family computer and announced it publicly. I told myself I wanted to die. That's what exposure does to a shame that runs underground: it doesn't kill the shame, it feeds it. My father brought it up again later, during a motorcycle accident recovery when I was sedated and unguarded, and he hit me. I remember thinking: if you wanted to hurt me, you found the wound.

At school I was never what the report cards said I was. I led my cricket team. I raised my voice when a teacher hit students, and got hit for it myself. There was a girl I liked from Class 6 all the way through Class 12, quiet and consistent, mine to carry alone. I shared my sister's paintings as my own to get recognition. I am not proud of that, but I understand it: I was a boy who needed to be seen, in a house where there weren't enough eyes turned the right direction.

Class 10: I did not study for the entire year. Then, in the final two months, I focused with something I can only describe as fire compressed into a point. I scored better than most of the students who had worked all year. I did this not because I was disciplined, but because I understood, even at fifteen, that I had reserves others were not accounting for. The pattern was real: long fallow periods followed by sudden surges that surprised everyone except me.

In Class 11, I got chickenpox during exams and scored 95.5%. My father stopped doubting me after that. In Class 12, I told him my score before I sat the exam, attended half the classes, and hit exactly what I predicted. I challenged the existence of God and believed I could shape my own destiny to a significant degree.

I was not wrong about my capacity. I was completely unprepared for what the world had been quietly organizing while I was busy proving myself to classrooms.

Part Three: Delhi and the Doors That Opened

In 2009, I walked into St. Stephen's College, Delhi — one of India's finest institutions — from a humble background, with all the confidence in the world and none of the social fluency that the institution assumed. The classmates around me were heading to foreign internships, moving in worlds built on inherited access. I stayed in cheap accommodation because I refused to ask my father for more. That decision cost me my regulation.

In my first test, I scored zero. I was banned from class for a week. I responded by stopping attendance entirely.

Then my father bought me a camera.

The camera found something in me that the institution hadn't touched. I became a photographer. My college photo went viral on Facebook. I was known by name before people had put a face to it. I joined the Photography Society and encountered a well-known photographer who occupied a particular position in my college life — appearing kind at first, connected to political circles, later revealing layers of pride and ego I would spend years untangling. What his actual agenda was, I still cannot say with certainty. I only know that I walked into those circles believing in the common currencies of authenticity and mentorship, and I learned slowly that neither of those was what was on offer.

I failed one subject in Year 1. Lost my camera on a trip. Organized a photo exhibition to raise money for a replacement — it fell short, and I fell into desperate ways to make it work, lost money, repaid friends from my own funds. I failed Year 2 twice.

Then I met a girlfriend — or something positioned as one. She framed our relationship as "good friendship even when we were making out." She never acknowledged my photography. She made demeaning comments. She never introduced me as her boyfriend. I was inside the relationship and perpetually outside of it, which is its own kind of surgical damage. I only cleared Year 2 on my third attempt after she entered my life — same surge pattern, different fuel.

In 2013, I started cannabis. A close friend introduced me, in the same period when my father's health was destabilizing and I was growing sick of the relationship and what it was doing to me. A new world opened. I loved the solitude. I watched films, read philosophy, thought in directions I had never had access to before. It felt like expansion.

It was also the beginning of everything that would break me.

My father was hospitalised for alcoholic liver disease. He nearly died, was stabilized, was supposed to go home in two days. Then something happened during that recovery — an episode that raised questions I spent years trying to answer before I finally set them down. I only work with what I have now, toward the life I am building.

My father died on June 1, 2014. I was 23. I had six pending exams and sat for one.

I was dropped into a tank full of sharks.

Part Four: The Tank

Let me be clear about what the tank was: a 23-year-old man, newly fatherless, in a household of women who did not fully understand the practical battles now required, surrounded by people who had opinions and no resources, fighting inheritance systems, land disputes, and debt, while the family narrative against me was already being written by people who had seen me drinking and smoking in the days after my father's death. To them: spoiled brat. To me: a man who had just lost his father and was carrying everything alone while doing it visibly, messily, and without the performance of dignified grief they wanted from me.

I managed my father's retirement funds. My mother and I cleared substantial loans over two years. I navigated inheritance paperwork, land disputes, and politically connected enemies, largely without help, under constant criticism from people who contributed none.

A family member pressured me into selling land my father and I had loved. That land was not just property. It was orientation. And I had no one steady to stand next to me when it went.

My girlfriend revealed she had been seeing the man I had long suspected and directly confronted her about. She had denied it. Now she was with him openly. I expressed suicidal tendencies to her — not as intention, but as the sound a person makes when they are being erased. She called my sister. I came home. Then she kept reaching back while remaining with him, recollecting our conversations differently than they occurred, giving me hope and withdrawing it. That is not a failed relationship. That is a campaign.

A close friend since childhood crashed my car on a forced trip and refused to pay. Then he withdrew. I faced the consequences alone.

By 2015, cannabis was constant. I sat beside a canal once and considered whether to stay. I came out of it with a philosophy, not the one offered by anyone else: death is inevitable and life is a choice to see if things improve. I chose life. I want to name that choice clearly, without drama: I chose to stay. And I would keep choosing that, in various forms, for the decade that followed.

In 2016, with loans cleared by selling land I loved, I returned to Delhi. I used cannabis heavily. I went to sex workers. I threw money at friends who criticized me for it. My ex came back and I rejected her. Through overlapping circles from college, I re-engaged with a therapist who used intentional philosophical tests in place of conventional therapy — I was walking into something I did not yet have the map for.

Part Five: The First Break — 2017

Paranoia builds the way water pressure builds. It finds the cracks that were already there.

There was something about the water in the building. Something about the key my photography mentor had to my flat. Something about the way the political connections between various figures in my life kept aligning in patterns that felt too structured to be coincidence. The pressure had been accumulating for years: my father's death, the debts, the betrayals, the relationship, the abandoned friendship, the land disputes, the relentless isolation. And I was heavy on cannabis.

In May 2017, the pressure found the crack.

I ran from the apartment complex into the street and ended up at a police station. I posted every suspicion in real time on Facebook. I publicly accused my mother and family of intentionally harming my father. That accusation had a real root — I had learned that family members had secretly given my father a substance meant to curb his drinking, a practice with cultural precedent. The psychosis took that real root and grew something monstrous from it. I know this now. The paranoid belief was not the truth of what happened. The cannabis and the grief and the accumulated years of powerlessness turned a real signal into a system of persecution that did not exist as I described it.

A family member came to my rescue in Delhi, brought me to Hyderabad, and had me hospitalised. One month. ECT — two sessions. The first fifteen days erased from memory. I was heavily sedated and aggressively trying to escape.

When I came out, I was stable, coherent, and profoundly ashamed. Let me hold those two things together: what I did was shameful, and I was stable. Both were true.

I moved to Hyderabad. I exercised. I kept doctor appointments. I stayed off cannabis and cigarettes for six months.

Then I relapsed.

I told myself it was dose-dependent — I had read that somewhere. I started using secretly, in small amounts. Then daily. I hated the control. When they refused to let me return to Delhi, I threatened suicide again. They let me go.

Back in Delhi, I used heavily, stopped medication, had intermittent delusional thoughts. I re-engaged with the therapist. I suspected my flatmates were "captured by a secret group." I imagined snipers pointed at my building and contemplated jumping to escape threats that did not exist outside my mind.

I came back to Hyderabad in 2019.

I am not going to dress up the relapse or explain it away. I made choices I had no business making. I knew the risk and I chose anyway because the alternative — accepting that the substance I loved was the enemy — was a loss I was not yet ready to acknowledge.

Part Six: The Second Break — 2019, and What the Facility Did to Me

A few days without cannabis in early 2019, and the pattern returned. I believed I was Lord Shiva himself. I believed my movements were tracked by secret spiritual groups. I believed that everyone I had encountered since 2010 was part of an interlocking network, testing me for recruitment.

This time I went to the hospital voluntarily. After that: no memory. I was aggressive, I tried to run, I hit my mother, I stripped naked as protest to be released, I reportedly jumped a fifteen-foot gate.

ECT again. Two more sessions, four total across both episodes. That month is gone.

What I remember is the shame. For hitting my mother. For what I put her through. I carry that.

My mother, in a situation of genuine helplessness, placed me in a residential de-addiction facility. I need to say what happened there plainly, because the record has to be accurate:

I was tied to a bed for approximately fourteen days. I was force-medicated. The counsellors were not trained in psychology. There was no real therapy. There was no channel for what I actually felt. If I expressed resentment or disagreement, I was afraid they would keep me longer or make my conditions harder. I stayed five months.

I did not complain while I was there because the guilt about hitting my mother kept me quiet. That guilt was real, and those who ran the facility knew it and used it. The two things — my genuine shame and their genuine abuse — ran simultaneously.

I came out sober. I stayed sober for more than two years.

What I want to say to anyone who thinks confinement is treatment: confinement enforced by people who cannot name what they are treating, who increase medication when a patient expresses disagreement, who use guilt as a management tool — that is not treatment. That is control dressed up in the language of care. And I know the difference now because I spent years in it.

Part Seven: The Chain and What It Cost

After the second episode, I did the right things for a while. I exercised, ate well, did online courses, rebuilt partially. Then a stressful situation with my sister triggered cigarettes. Six months later, cannabis again.

I have mapped the chain now because I needed to understand it to stop fearing it and start outrunning it:

Cigarettes are the SPOF — the single point of failure. Not cannabis. Cannabis is the disaster. But cigarettes are the door that opens to cannabis, and the door opens through four entry points: stress, loneliness and sexual frustration, boredom, and — the one I only discovered recently — feeling accomplished. Success is a trigger. The dopamine of achievement opens the same door as the dopamine of the substance. That is not a failure of character. It is a neurological fact I need to build around.

The chain also runs through pornography. The conditioned loop started at fourteen: arousal, secrecy, shame. By the time I was a young adult, cannabis and pornography had formed their own feedback circuit. They were never separate systems. They were one system, each node reinforcing the others, all of it feeding the same underlying wound.

In 2023, a third episode. I had been using cannabis after a period of sobriety, then quit abruptly for a week. The withdrawal triggered something. I believed I was awakened and spiritually special. I believed I was conversing with a technology billionaire through a mutual friend's chat. I believed he was coming to India to honour my intelligence. I felt connected to everything.

What broke through the psychosis in that episode was a call with the therapist I had been in contact with intermittently for years. I told her I was ready to take the pain to know the truth and someone had to. She had made me question my own positions for years. That day I held mine. I cried afterward, in the car, thinking about what I had said about pain. I felt, for the first time in a long time, like I had stood up.

Then my mother placed me in the same facility. Four months, this time.

After discharge, I smoked approximately five joints in the following year. I drank four Red Bulls once and had temporary delusional thoughts lasting hours. That data point settled the question permanently: my vulnerability is structural, not dose-dependent. The brake system is gone. This is not a working hypothesis. This is what happened to me.

Part Eight: The Accident — November 6, 2024

For fifteen days before it happened, something in my system was running in a mode I can now recognize: elevated noise, urgency, aberrant salience. I had used cannabis perhaps five times total in all of 2024. But something was building.

I was driving toward a holy place. The morning sun was present. A lake. Greenery. Dried trees. I felt a beauty so total that my eyes closed on their own. I did namaskar to the creator with full conviction. I released the steering wheel.

The next thing I remember: standing behind the car, covered in blood, people around me, an ambulance already there.

I will hold what happened through multiple lenses simultaneously, because that is the honest way to hold it: it was a psychotic episode, a trance state, possibly dissociative. I was in a medical emergency. I also believe something took over in those seconds — that I surrendered to something and was not killed, and that this was not a random fact. I believe I was put here to improve the environment that broke me.

My mother found me and admitted me to the facility.

This time I was there for eleven months.

Part Nine: The Eleventh Month

The fourth stay was the longest and the worst.

Heavily medicated for the first two months. Isolated from all family contact for the first six months. Scar tissue on my face from the accident that received no proper attention during confinement. No sunlight for eleven months. Forced classes, forced television, forced exercise schedules, with no channel for what I actually felt. When I finally wrote an angry letter at the six-month mark and confronted the facility's owner about inadequate treatment, he manhandled me physically. My medications were increased when I pushed back. The counsellors were untrained. There were others being held there without any symptoms at all.

At least there will be more freedom in jail. I mean that literally, not rhetorically.

Here is what confinement of that kind does: it does not teach you to be free. It teaches you to perform the behaviour required to be released. And I am telling you this clearly so that no one misunderstands what that facility was and is.

My sister fought to bring me out. My mother wanted me to stay another year. My sister won.

After discharge, I filed a complaint with the National Human Rights Commission against the facility. I did this for myself and for the others held without cause and for whoever would come after us. This was not a dramatic gesture. This was the minimum that honesty required.

Part Ten: The System I Was Born Into

Let me say clearly what the backdrop of every episode has been: a family system that I did not choose and could not leave, with no neutral adult to stand in the gap.

My father was the financial backbone. When he died, I became the backbone with none of the authority and all of the obligation. My sister married within three months of his death and left me with the debts and disputes. She would later return after her marriage collapsed, moving back in with her daughter, and the household became what it has been since: volatile, dense with unprocessed conflict, without stable borders between people's lives.

My niece is six years old. I have nightmares about her wellbeing. The house feels empty without her in it.

My mother has been my primary caregiver through every episode. I am genuinely grateful for that. She has also enabled dysfunction, lied to me despite being asked not to, and aligned with the family's narrative about my instability even as my predictions about our situations kept proving accurate. Her confidence in me eroded through the hospitalisations, and that erosion was used against me by everyone in the system who needed a reason to manage me rather than hear me.

And underneath all of it runs a quieter, daily damage. My mother and my sister land, again and again, on the two wounds that cut deepest — truth denied and manipulation. Truth denied: my reality waved off, my competence waved off, my history held up as proof that nothing I see can be trusted — so the clearer I get, the more they read it as instability. Manipulation: moved by agendas no one will name, triangulated, my own perception turned against me. And this is the cruelty of it. Gaslighting strikes the exact faculty my illness already attacks — my hold on what is real. When the people closest to me deny my truth and steer me by hidden hands, they are not just hurting me. They are pressing on the one place I am most breakable. I name it now, plainly, because naming it is how I refuse to drown in it.

My grandmother was my most stable caregiver through my formative years in Classes 6 through 10. She cooked, cleaned, kept the house, and gave me the closest thing to reliable ground I had. Later she became a source of guilt and manipulation — spreading narratives, infantilizing me, asking why I can't fix what other people break. She treats me as a loser. I understand she is 84 and frightened. I do not accept her assessment.

A cousin entered my life after the first episode and consistently fed the thing I most needed to guard against — the search for hidden meaning, the spiritual and superstitious frames. I have learned to hold him at a distance.

My closest friend abandoned me. My ex-girlfriend kept returning to wound me. My photography mentor's actual agenda I still cannot fully name. The therapist — I have forgiven her, but I understand I cannot work with her. She triggers the wound of truth denied.

The people who talked about integrity first showed their real faces last.

I was blind, for a long time, to the attention and emotional regulation economy that underlies most human social behaviour. I thought I was dealing in truth and expected it returned. We are in an attention and emotional regulation economy. All other currencies are counterfeit. I know that now. I live accordingly.

Part Eleven: Paranoid Delusions with Real Roots

I need to say something precisely about what happened to my mind.

The paranoid delusions were not random. Their content was drawn from real events — there genuinely were connections between figures in my life who turned out to know each other. There genuinely were disputes involving possible fraud in systems around me. Cannabis and stress and grief blew these real events out of proportion into a system of persecution that did not exist as I described it.

Here is how I understand it: paranoid delusions which I know are not real, but whose roots were in real events in my life, blown out of proportion by cannabis and accumulated stress and the feeling of being powerless, unworthy, and stripped of meaning. The psychosis was not fabrication. It was a distorted amplification of a real signal running through a system with no way to discharge. The problem was the amplification. The problem was the cannabis. The problem was the unprocessed grief and the years of accumulated betrayal and the complete absence of any safe channel for what I actually felt.

My single, identified prodrome — the one warning sign I can detect and take seriously — is this: looking for hidden meaning. Not spiritual intensity. Not racing thoughts. Not certainty. Not disrupted sleep. Those are triggers, and I account for them, but they are not the tell. The tell is aberrant salience. When I start finding patterns in things that don't quite connect. When I start reading significance into coincidences. That is the flag. That is when I need to act.

I know myself. I know the flag. That knowledge was built at enormous cost and I will not waste it.

Part Twelve: The Frameworks I Built Because the Institutions Failed Me

I failed institutions, people failed me, substances destroyed me — so I built my own way of understanding myself. I call it Symmetra. The full version is an essay I wrote: "You Forgot. That's the Game."

The heart of it is simple. Consciousness isn't something you reach. It's already what you are — the watcher behind everything. The ego is not that watcher. It's the operator that forgot what it was. The whole game is forgetting, and coming back.

For years I tried to rise — to escape upward into pure awareness. It nearly killed me. Pure awareness alone is dead air. The life is in the middle: the body, the world, the centre held in tune. The direction was never up. It is back to centre, again and again. The descent is the awakening.

There is one gauge. Grip. Open hand is home. Closed fist is drift. And it fails exactly when I am gripped — so my checks live outside me, written while I'm clear. Keep the hardware: sleep, food, movement. Hold everything lightly, including this. Notice everything. Fight nothing. And notice the grip.

Part Thirteen: The Substances, Finally Settled

On cannabis: I will never use again. This is not a goal or a resolution. It is a settled fact. My vulnerability is now structural. Five uses across an entire year was enough to contribute to the fourth episode. The brake system no longer exists. I am not a person who can use cannabis in any amount. I am not mourning this loudly, but I will not pretend it is not a loss. It is. The thing that once felt like expansion was always also the thing that would eventually take me all the way under. Both were true from the beginning.

On cigarettes: I have not quit yet. I am taking it slow, tapering with nicotine patches. My father died twelve years ago on June 1st. I named my quit timer after that anniversary as a tribute to him. Breaking the generational pattern is the aim.

On pornography: I am working on this. The conditioned pathway was trained at fourteen. The loop — arousal, secrecy, shame — is deeply grooved. I named my quit timer "I chose real connection," because without real connection, all of that is temporary and makes the loneliness worse, not better.

On medication: Aripiprazole 1.25 mg. I am extremely sensitive to serotonin and dopamine medications. Small dose changes alter my behaviour noticeably. I am tapering, slowly, under psychiatric supervision, toward eventually coming off entirely if stability holds. I know from the experience of skipping doses that the vulnerability is still real. I am honest with myself about this.

I have not had cannabis since October 24, 2024 — Kaalashtami. I framed it as a spiritual act because it needed to feel like more than just quitting. It needed to feel like a reclamation.

Part Fourteen: Where I Am Now

I am 35 years old — 36 running. I live in Huzurabad with my mother, grandmother, sister, and niece. I have been the only man in this household since 2014. I want to name that without drama: twelve years as the sole man in an all-female household, managing grief and land disputes and debts and hospitalizations and the daily friction of people who are all wound around each other's pain. I did not collapse. I bent, badly, more than once. But I did not collapse.

I run a technology and content studio. I have worked with brand clients. I have built systems from scratch when no template existed. The work is real.

I am fighting a land dispute in the Telangana revenue system — my father's land — against what I believe is a small syndicate of politically connected operators and corrupt officials who stripped the digital records after my father died. I have filed RTIs, grievances, and approached multiple levels of the bureaucracy. The High Court petition is already filed. This land is part of my father's legacy. I am not done.

I have filed a Human Rights complaint against the facility. The follow-up waits until the higher-priority work is done — but it is filed. For myself. For the others held without cause. For whoever comes after us.

I am pursuing matrimony. After more than a decade without real human physical intimacy, that longing is real and I will not perform indifference to it. I carry insecurity from betrayal. I also carry the clearest understanding I have ever had of what I actually need and what I will not accept.

I am building a startup — a virtual tours platform for Indian real estate and hospitality that I believe can scale into something significant. The tagline is "Clarity First." My professional philosophy is a direct extension of my personal philosophy: eliminate urgency, salience, manipulation by design. Build systems that protect attention.

I am not at the bottom. I am not at the top. I am in the middle of something that is still being built.

You cannot recover in the same environment that broke you. I know this precisely. I am working on the geometry of exit.

Part Fifteen: On Being Seen — and on What I Am

The wound that runs through everything — from childhood to college to the episodes to the facility to the years of managing the family system alone — is the wound of being unseen.

I never thought I had the kind of resources that would attract people with agendas. I thought I was dealing in truth with everyone. I was not blind because I was stupid. I was blind because I believed in the currencies I was trading in — authenticity, intelligence, real feeling — and I assumed others were trading in the same.

They were not, many of them. Not always. And when I finally understood the depth of how much I had been unseen, suppressed, used — my system overloaded. The grandiosity in psychosis, I understand now, was compensatory: now you see me, now you know what I am. The swings between feeling worthless and feeling chosen were two sides of one coin, both generated by the same underlying deprivation.

Here is what I actually am, beneath the episodes and the relapses and the confinements and the shame:

I am a systems thinker. I am a photographer. I see life in everything. I diagnosed my mother's spinal infection when the doctors were still prescribing the wrong therapy. I have navigated bureaucratic systems, legal systems, and family systems simultaneously, alone, while managing my own medication and recovery. I have built philosophical frameworks that serious people have engaged with seriously. I have built tools because I needed them to exist and no one else was going to build them.

I am a work in progress, and I have something to contribute to this world. These two things are not contradictions. They are the same sentence.

Enough negotiating with the ground.

What I want is not small, but it is not grand either. I want stability. I want clarity. I want a partner. I want financial independence. I want a home that is mine. I want to care for my mother and grandmother from a position of strength rather than trapped obligation. And I want to build things — in technology, in philosophy, in the world — that protect people from the kind of manipulation and coercion and invisible erasure I have lived through.

Truth is not blinded by fear, insecurity, or selfishness. It is selfless love. You get what you give in this world.

I chose life at a canal in 2015. I chose it again every day I came out of those facilities. I choose it now, with eyes open, in a body that has been through the fire and come out scarred and clear.

I am Logan. I am Vishwanath. Both are true. Both are here.

I choose it still.
